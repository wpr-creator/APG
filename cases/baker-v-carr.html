Baker v. Carr
369 U.S. 186 · March 26, 1962 · Justice William Brennan
Unit 5 Equal Protection 14th Amendment ⭐ Frequently Tested
📋
BACKGROUND

Tennessee had not redrawn its legislative districts since 1901 — more than 60 years. In that time, massive population shifts had moved millions from rural areas to cities. By 1962, a rural district with 2,340 voters had the same representation in the state legislature as an urban district with 133,000 voters. Charles Baker, a Memphis resident, sued Tennessee Secretary of State Joe Carr, arguing the malapportionment violated the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment.

The problem was that earlier courts had refused to touch apportionment cases — labeling them "political questions" beyond judicial reach. In Colegrove v. Green (1946), Justice Frankfurter had famously warned courts to stay out of the "political thicket" of redistricting. Baker asked the Court to step in anyway — and it did, in one of the most consequential jurisdictional decisions in history.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Do federal courts have jurisdiction to hear challenges to state legislative apportionment, or are such questions "political questions" beyond judicial reach?
⚖️
THE RULING
✓ Federal courts CAN hear apportionment cases — 6 to 2
Majority: Brennan · Warren · Black · Douglas · Clark · Stewart  |  Dissent: Frankfurter · Harlan
"The right asserted is within the reach of judicial protection under the Fourteenth Amendment."
The Court held that apportionment challenges are justiciable — courts can hear them. This was not a ruling on the merits of Tennessee's districts; it only said the federal courts had jurisdiction. But that was revolutionary. Brennan developed a multi-factor test for when a question is truly "political" and off-limits to courts, and apportionment didn't meet it. The door was now open. Baker set the stage for Reynolds v. Sims (1964), which established "one person, one vote" as a constitutional requirement for all legislative districts.
📐
PRINCIPLE ESTABLISHED
Courts Can Review Apportionment — "One Person, One Vote" Begins Here
Legislative apportionment is a justiciable question under the Equal Protection Clause — federal courts have the power and duty to hear challenges to malapportioned districts. Baker did not itself mandate equal districts, but it opened the courthouse door. Reynolds v. Sims (1964) walked through that door and required that both chambers of state legislatures be apportioned by population. Together Baker and Reynolds created the modern framework for redistricting law.
⚡
THE DISSENT — JUSTICE FRANKFURTER
Justice Frankfurter (joined by Justice Harlan)
Frankfurter's dissent was one of his most passionate — he had spent his career warning courts away from political questions. He argued the majority was dragging courts into the inherently political business of drawing district lines, a task for legislatures and voters, not judges. Courts have no manageable standards for apportionment and no democratic legitimacy to impose their own preferences. "There is not under our Constitution a judicial remedy for every political mischief." His warning proved partially prophetic — redistricting litigation has become one of the most contested and politically charged areas of constitutional law.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • Reynolds v. Sims
    1964    Built directly on Baker — held that BOTH chambers of state legislatures must be apportioned by population. "Legislators represent people, not trees or acres." One person, one vote made explicit.
  • Shaw v. Reno
    1993    Applied Equal Protection to racial gerrymandering — race cannot be the predominant factor in drawing districts even if the purpose is to help minority voters. Uses the same Equal Protection framework Baker opened up.
  • Rucho v. Common Cause
    2019    The Court held that PARTISAN gerrymandering is a political question — courts cannot hear challenges to maps drawn for partisan advantage. Baker's justiciability holding doesn't extend to partisanship, only population equality and racial fairness.
  • Modern ImpactEvery redistricting cycle following each census triggers Baker-based litigation. After the 2020 census, dozens of states faced legal challenges. Baker created an entire field of election law that fills federal dockets every decade.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Baker is almost always tested alongside Shaw v. Reno — both involve Equal Protection challenges to districting, but Baker is about justiciability (can courts hear the case?) while Shaw is about the merits (racial gerrymandering violates Equal Protection). Know that Baker opened the door; Reynolds v. Sims established one person, one vote; Shaw applied it to race.

Common Mistakes
✗Saying Baker established "one person, one vote" — that was Reynolds v. Sims (1964).
✗Thinking Baker ruled Tennessee's districts unconstitutional — it only said courts could hear the challenge.
✗Confusing Baker (apportionment justiciability) with Shaw (racial gerrymandering merits).
