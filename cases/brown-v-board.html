Unit 3 · Civil Rights · 14th Amendment · Equal Protection · EK 3.3.A
Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka
347 U.S. 483 · May 17, 1954 · Chief Justice Earl Warren
Unit 3 14th Amendment Equal Protection ⭐ Most Important Civil Rights Case
📋
BACKGROUND

In 1951, Oliver Brown's daughter Linda was denied admission to Sumner Elementary School in Topeka, Kansas — a white school seven blocks from their home — because she was Black. She had to walk past Sumner to catch a bus to Monroe Elementary, the Black school across town. The NAACP, led by Thurgood Marshall, consolidated Linda Brown's case with four similar cases from Delaware, South Carolina, Virginia, and Washington D.C., all challenging the "separate but equal" doctrine established in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896).

Plessy had held that racial segregation was constitutional as long as the separate facilities were equal. In practice, Black schools were systematically underfunded and inferior. Marshall's legal strategy, informed by social science research by psychologist Kenneth Clark, attacked not just the inequality of facilities but the premise of separation itself — that segregated schools could ever be truly equal.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Does racial segregation in public schools — even when the physical facilities and other tangible factors are equal — violate the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment?
⚖️
THE RULING
✓ Segregation unconstitutional — Unanimous (9-0)
Unanimous · Chief Justice Earl Warren · May 17, 1954
"We conclude that, in the field of public education, the doctrine of 'separate but equal' has no place. Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal."
Warren — who had quietly lobbied fellow justices to make the ruling unanimous, knowing a divided Court would invite defiance — wrote a deliberately short, accessible opinion designed to be understood by ordinary Americans. The core holding: segregation by race in public schools generates a feeling of inferiority in Black children that undermines their educational development. "Separate but equal" is a constitutional fiction — separation itself creates inequality. The psychological harm of segregation is as real as the physical disparity in facilities. Plessy v. Ferguson was overruled. Warren achieved unanimity by keeping the opinion narrow and scheduling a second round of argument (Brown II) on the remedy.
📐
PRINCIPLE ESTABLISHED
Separate Is Inherently Unequal — Equal Protection Prohibits Racial Segregation in Public Education
Racial segregation in public schools violates the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment — regardless of whether the physical facilities are materially equal. The act of separating children by race generates a feeling of inferiority that cannot be undone by equal buildings or equal curricula. "Separate but equal" (Plessy 1896) is therefore unconstitutional. Brown applies directly to public education; subsequent decisions and civil rights legislation extended this principle to all areas of public life. This case launched the Civil Rights Movement and is widely considered the most important Supreme Court decision of the 20th century.
⚡
THE RESISTANCE AND BROWN II
Brown II (1955) and "Massive Resistance"
There was no dissent in Brown I — but there was massive resistance across the South. A year later, in Brown II, the Court ordered desegregation to proceed "with all deliberate speed" — a vague standard that many Southern school districts used to delay for years or decades. 101 Southern congressmen signed the "Southern Manifesto" condemning Brown as a "clear abuse of judicial power." President Eisenhower had to send the 82nd Airborne to Little Rock in 1957 to enforce desegregation of Central High School. Many Southern districts did not meaningfully desegregate until the late 1960s and 1970s — nearly two decades after Brown.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • Overturned
    Plessy (1896)    Plessy v. Ferguson had established "separate but equal" in 1896. Brown demolished it 58 years later. Know both cases — the AP exam frequently asks about the relationship between them and what changed.
  • Letter from
    Birmingham Jail    King explicitly invoked Brown and the 14th Amendment in his argument for civil disobedience. Brown gave the Civil Rights Movement its constitutional foundation — demonstrating that the courts could be an instrument of racial justice.
  • Civil Rights Act
    1964    Brown desegregated schools through the courts; the Civil Rights Act extended equal protection to employment, public accommodations, and federally funded programs. Brown made the Act politically possible by establishing the constitutional principle.
  • Thurgood MarshallMarshall argued Brown before the Court and won. He later became the first African American Supreme Court Justice (1967-1991). His career — from NAACP attorney to Supreme Court Justice — is one of the great arcs in American legal history.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Brown is the most important civil rights case and appears on virtually every AP Government exam in some form — as direct evidence in an essay, as the ruling being compared to another case, or as the background for a Civil Rights Movement question. Know: (1) it overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896), (2) 'separate but equal has no place,' (3) it was unanimous, (4) Thurgood Marshall argued it. Also know Brown II and why desegregation was slow.

Common Mistakes
✗Confusing Brown I (separate is unequal) with Brown II (desegregate with 'all deliberate speed') — know both.
✗Forgetting that Brown overturned PLESSY V. FERGUSON (1896) — always name the case it overturned.
✗Saying desegregation happened quickly after Brown — it took decades in many districts, with massive resistance.
