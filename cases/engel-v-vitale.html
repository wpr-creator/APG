Engel v. Vitale
370 U.S. 421 · June 25, 1962 · Justice Hugo Black
Unit 3 1st Amendment Establishment Clause ⭐ Frequently Tested
📋
BACKGROUND

The New York State Board of Regents composed a brief, non-denominational prayer for use in public schools: "Almighty God, we acknowledge our dependence upon Thee, and we beg Thy blessings upon us, our parents, our teachers and our country." The prayer was voluntary — students who didn't want to participate could remain silent or leave the room. The Herricks School District in New Hyde Park adopted it. Steven Engel and four other parents sued William Vitale, the school board president, arguing the prayer violated the Establishment Clause.

The state court upheld the prayer. The Supreme Court reversed — in a ruling that sparked immediate national controversy. Critics flooded Congress with mail. Some called it the most hated Supreme Court decision in history. It remains deeply contested more than 60 years later.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Does the recitation of a state-composed prayer in public schools violate the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment — even if the prayer is voluntary and non-denominational?
⚖️
THE RULING
✓ School prayer unconstitutional — 6 to 1
Majority: Black · Warren · Douglas · Clark · Harlan · Brennan  |  Dissent: Stewart
"It is no part of the business of government to compose official prayers for any group of the American people to recite as a part of a religious program carried on by government."
The Court held that the Establishment Clause prohibits government from composing official prayers, even if participation is voluntary and the prayer is non-denominational. The fact that the prayer was written by a government body — the State Board of Regents — was the constitutional problem. Government-composed prayer in government schools is state-sponsored religion regardless of how brief or neutral the prayer may be. The voluntary nature didn't save it: in a school setting, students face significant pressure to conform, making "voluntary" prayer coercive in practice.
📐
PRINCIPLE ESTABLISHED
Government-Composed Prayer Violates the Establishment Clause
The government may not write or sponsor prayers for use in public schools. This applies whether the prayer is: voluntary or mandatory, denominational or non-denominational, long or brief. The Establishment Clause erects a "wall of separation" between government and religion. Schools cannot require, encourage, or endorse prayer as a governmental act — though students retain their individual First Amendment right to pray privately. The Lemon test (1971) later formalized the Establishment Clause framework: government action must have a secular purpose, neither advance nor inhibit religion, and avoid excessive entanglement with religion.
⚡
THE DISSENT — JUSTICE STEWART
Justice Stewart (sole dissenter)
Stewart argued the majority was not protecting religious freedom but "misapplying a great constitutional principle" by denying schoolchildren the opportunity to share in the spiritual heritage of the nation. He pointed to official government religious expressions — the national motto "In God We Trust," the President's inaugural prayer, chaplains in Congress — as evidence that the Founders never intended to banish religion from public life entirely. A truly voluntary, non-coercive prayer recitation in schools, he argued, was no different from these accepted practices.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • Abington v. Schempp
    1963    Extended Engel — struck down Bible reading in public schools. Together Engel and Schempp established that government-sponsored religious exercises in public schools are unconstitutional.
  • Lee v. Weisman
    1992    Clergy-led graduation prayer at public school unconstitutional — the "coercion test." Even if technically voluntary, students face social pressure to participate in a school-sponsored ceremony.
  • Santa Fe v. Doe
    2000    Student-led prayer over school PA system before football games unconstitutional — still government-sponsored because the school controls the microphone and the event.
  • Kennedy v. Bremerton
    2022    The Court allowed a public school football coach to pray on the field after games — distinguishing private religious expression (protected) from school-sponsored prayer (prohibited). Engel remains good law but the lines are being redrawn.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Engel is tested most often as the foundational school prayer case. Know the key distinction: government-composed or government-sponsored prayer is unconstitutional; individual student prayer is protected. The AP exam frequently presents scenarios involving graduation prayers, moment of silence, or student religious clubs — apply Engel for the government action question.

Common Mistakes
✗Thinking Engel bans all prayer in schools — it bans government-sponsored prayer. Students can pray privately.
✗Forgetting the prayer was VOLUNTARY — the Court ruled it unconstitutional anyway. Voluntariness didn't matter.
✗Confusing Establishment Clause (no state religion) with Free Exercise Clause (can't prohibit religion).
