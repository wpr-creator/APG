Gideon v. Wainwright
372 U.S. 335 · March 18, 1963 · Justice Hugo Black
Unit 3 6th Amendment Right to Counsel ⭐ Selective Incorporation
📋
BACKGROUND

Clarence Earl Gideon was a 51-year-old drifter with a 5th grade education when he was charged with breaking and entering a Florida pool hall in 1961. He couldn't afford a lawyer. He asked the Florida court to appoint one. The judge refused — Florida law only provided appointed counsel in capital (death penalty) cases. Gideon represented himself, was convicted, and sentenced to five years. From his prison cell, he wrote a five-page handwritten petition to the Supreme Court in pencil. The Court accepted it.

The case overturned Betts v. Brady (1942), which had held that the 6th Amendment right to counsel was not a fundamental right automatically required of states. Gideon's case — argued before the Supreme Court by future Justice Abe Fortas — produced one of the most important unanimous decisions in history.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Does the 6th Amendment's guarantee of the right to counsel in criminal proceedings apply to state courts through the 14th Amendment — requiring states to provide attorneys to defendants who cannot afford them?
⚖️
THE RULING
✓ States must provide counsel — Unanimous (9-0)
Unanimous · Justice Hugo Black
"Reason and reflection require us to recognize that in our adversary system of criminal justice, any person haled into court, who is too poor to hire a lawyer, cannot be assured a fair trial unless counsel is provided for him."
The Court unanimously overturned Betts v. Brady and held that the right to counsel is fundamental — incorporated against the states through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause. The adversarial system of criminal justice is built on the assumption that both sides have lawyers. Without a lawyer, an indigent defendant simply cannot participate meaningfully in their own defense. The government provides prosecutors; fairness requires it also provide defense counsel for those who can't afford it. Gideon was retried with a lawyer — and acquitted in about an hour.
📐
PRINCIPLE ESTABLISHED
Right to Counsel Is Fundamental — Applies to All States in Felony Cases
The 6th Amendment right to counsel is incorporated against the states through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause. Any person charged with a felony who cannot afford an attorney must be provided one by the state. A conviction obtained in violation of this right is unconstitutional. This is one of the clearest examples of selective incorporation — the process by which the Court has applied Bill of Rights protections to state governments one case at a time. Later cases extended Gideon: Argersinger v. Hamlin (1972) applied it to any offense resulting in imprisonment.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • Selective Incorporation
    Key Example    Gideon is the textbook example of selective incorporation — the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause applied a Bill of Rights protection (6th Amendment right to counsel) to state courts. Before Gideon, states could try felony defendants without providing lawyers. After Gideon, they couldn't.
  • Public Defender
    System Created    Gideon forced states to create or dramatically expand public defender offices. Today public defenders handle the majority of all criminal cases in the United States — representing millions of indigent defendants annually, often with crushing caseloads and inadequate resources.
  • Strickland v.
    Washington 1984    Established the right to effective counsel — not just any lawyer, but one who meets minimum competence standards. Defendants can challenge convictions if their lawyer's performance fell below professional norms AND the deficiency prejudiced the outcome.
  • Gideon's TrumpetAnthony Lewis wrote the definitive account of the case — "Gideon's Trumpet" (1964). Still widely taught in law schools as a model of how constitutional doctrine changes through individual cases. Clarence Gideon himself died in 1972, never seeing the full impact of his penciled petition.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Gideon is the key selective incorporation case for the 6th Amendment. Know that it: (1) overturned Betts v. Brady, (2) applied the right to counsel to states through the 14th Amendment Due Process Clause, and (3) requires counsel in ALL felony cases, not just capital cases. FRQ scenarios about a defendant without a lawyer at trial → Gideon applies.

Common Mistakes
✗Saying Gideon applies only to capital cases — it applies to ALL felony cases (and later, any offense with imprisonment).
✗Forgetting Gideon overturned Betts v. Brady (1942) — the prior rule was that counsel wasn't required in non-capital state cases.
✗Confusing Gideon (right to counsel) with Miranda (right to remain silent) — different amendments, different cases.
