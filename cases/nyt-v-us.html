New York Times Co. v. United States
403 U.S. 713 · June 30, 1971 · Per Curiam
Unit 3 1st Amendment Free Press ⭐ Prior Restraint — Key Concept
📋
BACKGROUND

June 1971. Vietnam War. Defense analyst Daniel Ellsberg leaked 7,000 pages of top-secret Defense Department history — the "Pentagon Papers" — to the New York Times and Washington Post. The documents revealed that the government had systematically deceived the public and Congress about the war's progress and prospects. The Nixon administration obtained court injunctions halting publication, arguing the documents' release would cause "grave and irreparable" harm to national security.

The case moved through the courts at extraordinary speed — the newspapers had been enjoined for only 15 days when the Supreme Court ruled. The decision came down 6-3, with six separate concurring opinions and three dissents, making the legal landscape complex. But the core holding was clear and historic.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Can the federal government use prior restraint — a court order preventing publication — to stop newspapers from publishing classified government documents, even when the government claims national security requires it?
⚖️
THE RULING
✗ Prior restraint unconstitutional — 6 to 3
For papers: Black · Douglas · Brennan · Stewart · White · Marshall  |  For government: Burger · Harlan · Blackmun
"Only a free and unrestrained press can effectively expose deception in government... The press was to serve the governed, not the governors."
The government failed to meet the "heavy burden" required to justify prior restraint — the most serious restriction on press freedom. Prior restraint is presumptively unconstitutional; the bar for allowing it is extraordinarily high. The government's vague claims of national security harm, without specific evidence of direct, immediate danger, were insufficient. Several justices noted the government could prosecute newspapers after publication under criminal law — but it could not prevent publication in advance. Publication proceeded the day of the ruling.
📐
PRINCIPLE ESTABLISHED
Prior Restraint Bears an Extraordinarily Heavy Presumption Against Constitutionality
Prior restraint — government censorship of speech or press before publication — carries the heaviest First Amendment burden. The government must show that publication would cause direct, immediate, and irreparable harm of the highest order. Vague or speculative national security claims do not meet this burden. This doesn't mean prior restraint is never constitutional — but the bar is so high that it almost never is. The press retains the right to publish what it obtains, even classified material, absent a showing of near-certain catastrophic harm.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • Schenck v. U.S.
    1919    Schenck allowed speech restriction during wartime with "clear and present danger." NYT v. U.S. shows the Court is far more protective of press freedom — especially against pre-publication restraint — than of individual speech during wartime.
  • Tinker v. Des Moines
    1969    Both cases involve government trying to stop speech before it reaches the public. Tinker protected student speech in schools; NYT protected press publication. Both establish that pre-censorship faces the highest constitutional burden.
  • Ellsberg & AftermathCriminal charges against Ellsberg were dropped after it emerged the Nixon administration had illegally broken into his psychiatrist's office. The Pentagon Papers ultimately had minimal operational security impact — validating the Court's skepticism of the government's harm claims.
  • Modern Press
    Freedom    NYT v. U.S. is cited in every major press freedom case. WikiLeaks, NSA surveillance disclosures, classified government leaks — all implicate the framework this case established. The tension between national security and press freedom it identified has never been fully resolved.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
NYT v. U.S. is the definitive prior restraint case — know that phrase cold. Prior restraint = government stopping publication before it happens. The Court said this bears a 'heavy burden' that the government almost never meets. FRQ scenarios involving government trying to stop a news story → apply this case. Key distinction: government can punish after publication; it almost cannot prevent publication.

Common Mistakes
✗Thinking prior restraint is always unconstitutional — the bar is very high but not impossible to clear.
✗Saying the government can never prosecute after publication — it can, just not restrain before.
✗Confusing this with Tinker — Tinker is about student speech; NYT is about press and prior restraint.
