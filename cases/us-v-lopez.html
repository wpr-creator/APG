United States v. Lopez
514 U.S. 549 · April 26, 1995 · Chief Justice William Rehnquist
Unit 2 Commerce Clause 10th Amendment ⭐ Frequently Tested
📋
BACKGROUND

Alfonso Lopez Jr. was a 12th grader in San Antonio who brought a .38 caliber handgun to school in 1992. He was charged under the Gun-Free School Zones Act of 1990 — a federal law passed by Congress making it a federal crime to possess a firearm within 1,000 feet of a school. Lopez challenged the law, arguing Congress had no constitutional authority to regulate guns near schools.

For 60 years after the New Deal, the Supreme Court had upheld virtually every use of the Commerce Clause, allowing Congress to regulate almost any economic activity. The government argued that guns near schools affect education, educated workers affect economic productivity, and economic productivity affects interstate commerce — therefore Congress could regulate gun possession near schools. The Court wasn't buying it. In a 5-4 decision, the Rehnquist Court drew a line for the first time in six decades.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Does the Gun-Free School Zones Act of 1990 — which makes it a federal crime to possess a firearm near a school — fall within Congress's power to regulate interstate commerce under Article I, Section 8?
⚖️
THE RULING
✗ Federal law struck down — 5 to 4
Majority: Rehnquist · O'Connor · Scalia · Kennedy · Thomas  |  Dissent: Stevens · Souter · Ginsburg · Breyer
"To uphold the Government's contentions here, we would have to pile inference upon inference in a manner that would bid fair to convert congressional authority under the Commerce Clause to a general police power of the sort retained by the States."
The Court held that the Act exceeded Congress's Commerce Clause authority. Rehnquist identified three categories Congress can regulate under the Commerce Clause: (1) the channels of interstate commerce (roads, waterways), (2) the instrumentalities of interstate commerce (vehicles, planes), and (3) activities that substantially affect interstate commerce. Carrying a gun near a school doesn't fit any category — it's not economic activity, and the connection to interstate commerce requires stacking inference on inference. That's too attenuated. If accepted, Congress could regulate anything, and the distinction between federal and state authority would disappear.
📐
THE LOPEZ THREE-CATEGORY TEST
Congress May Regulate Under the Commerce Clause:
Category 1 — The channels of interstate commerce (highways, rivers, railways, airspace)

Category 2 — The instrumentalities of interstate commerce and persons or things in interstate commerce (trucks, planes, trains, ships)

Category 3 — Activities that substantially affect interstate commerce — but only if the activity is economic or commercial in nature

Key limit: Non-economic activity (like carrying a gun) does not qualify under Category 3 even if it might indirectly affect commerce through a chain of inferences.
⚡
THE DISSENT — JUSTICE STEPHEN BREYER
Justice Breyer (joined by Stevens, Souter, Ginsburg)
The dissent argued that the majority's test was artificial — commerce is interconnected and Congress has empirical evidence linking gun violence near schools to educational disruption and economic harm. Educated workers drive the national economy; violence near schools undermines education; therefore the connection to interstate commerce is real, not speculative. The dissent accused the majority of imposing a judicial preference for state power rather than applying the Commerce Clause as written. Breyer would have deferred to Congress's judgment that a connection to commerce existed.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • McCulloch
    Contrast     McCulloch said congressional power is broad and flexible — "let the end be legitimate." Lopez said it has real limits — non-economic activity can't be regulated just because it might indirectly affect commerce. Together these cases define the floor and ceiling of federal power.
  • U.S. v. Morrison
    2000     The Court applied Lopez to strike down the Violence Against Women Act's civil remedy provision — Congress couldn't use the Commerce Clause for non-economic gender-motivated violence either. Lopez wasn't a one-off.
  • NFIB v. Sebelius
    2012     The Affordable Care Act case. Roberts held the individual mandate couldn't be justified under the Commerce Clause (following Lopez — not buying health insurance is inactivity, not economic activity) but survived as a tax. Lopez's framework shaped the entire debate.
  • 10th Amendment
    Federalism     Lopez was a victory for the 10th Amendment and states' rights. Regulating gun possession near schools is the kind of local police power traditionally reserved to states. The decision signaled the Court would actually enforce limits on federal power — not just defer to Congress indefinitely.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Lopez is almost always tested alongside McCulloch — the AP loves to ask you to compare them. McCulloch = broad federal power (implied powers, supremacy). Lopez = limits on federal power (Commerce Clause requires substantial effect on interstate commerce). Know the three Lopez categories cold. Know that Lopez was the first Commerce Clause limit in 60 years.

Why It Was Historic
First Commerce Clause limit since 1937 — 58 years of deference to Congress ended
Gun-Free School Zones Act re-passed with explicit Commerce Clause findings — survived second challenge
Common Mistakes
✗Saying Lopez overturned McCulloch — it didn't. It set limits on Commerce Clause, not implied powers generally.
✗Thinking guns near schools are now legal federally — Congress re-passed the law with commerce findings.
✗Forgetting this was 5-4 — a closely divided Court on a major federalism question.
