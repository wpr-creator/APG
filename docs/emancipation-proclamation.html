Emancipation Proclamation
President Abraham Lincoln · Executive Order · January 1, 1863
Unit 3 Required Document Civil War · Slavery
Historical Context — Read This First

By September 1862, the Civil War was nearly 18 months old and the Union was losing. The Confederate army had just driven Union forces back at the Second Battle of Bull Run. Britain and France were considering recognizing the Confederacy as a legitimate nation. Abraham Lincoln, who had insisted the war was about preserving the Union — not ending slavery — made a calculated decision to change that framing entirely.

On September 22, 1862, Lincoln issued a preliminary proclamation: unless the Confederate states returned to the Union by January 1, 1863, he would declare their enslaved people free. The Confederate states did not return. On January 1, 1863, Lincoln signed the Emancipation Proclamation. Read this document as a legal and political instrument, not as a moral declaration. Every word is deliberate — including the words that were left out.

I. The Legal Authority — Commander-in-Chief Power
Now, therefore I, Abraham Lincoln, President of the United States, by virtue of the power in me vested as Commander-in-Chief, of the Army and Navy of the United States in time of actual armed rebellion against the authority and government of the United States, and as a fit and necessary war measure for suppressing said rebellion, do, on this first day of January, in the year of our Lord one thousand eight hundred and sixty-three, order and declare that all persons held as slaves within said designated States, and parts of States, are, and henceforward shall be free.
War Powers Justification
Lincoln justified the Proclamation as a war measure — not as a moral statement about slavery. He acted under his commander-in-chief authority, not through Congress. This was deliberate: he believed he lacked constitutional authority to end slavery permanently by executive order, but could take military measures to deprive the Confederacy of its labor force. The legal authority was as a weapon of war, not a civil rights declaration.
⭐ AP Tested — Legal Basis
II. What It Did
The Proclamation freed enslaved people in the Confederate states — specifically the states "in rebellion" against the United States. It also authorized the enlistment of formerly enslaved men into the Union Army and Navy. By the end of the war, approximately 180,000 Black men had served in the Union Army, comprising about 10% of its total strength. The Proclamation also fundamentally changed the character of the war — Britain and France, both of which had already abolished slavery, could no longer consider recognizing the Confederacy without appearing to endorse slavery.
Three Effects
1. Freed enslaved people in rebel states (on paper — enforcement required Union military presence). 2. Authorized Black military service, which transformed the Union war effort. 3. Made European recognition of the Confederacy politically impossible — turning the war into an explicit conflict over slavery.
Strategic Impact
III. What It Didn't Do — The Critical Limits
The Proclamation applied only to Confederate states in rebellion. It explicitly exempted enslaved people in Union border states (Kentucky, Missouri, Maryland, Delaware) and in Confederate areas already under Union control. It also had no mechanism for enforcement in Confederate territory where Union forces had no presence. It did not grant citizenship to freed people, did not address the legal status of slavery in the United States generally, and was not a constitutional amendment — it was an executive order that could theoretically be reversed.
The Critical Limits
The Proclamation freed enslaved people where Lincoln had NO power to enforce it (Confederate states) and left them enslaved where he DID have power (Union states). This apparent contradiction reflects Lincoln's war-powers legal theory: he could only act against enemies, not against loyal states. This is why the 13th Amendment was necessary — to permanently abolish slavery everywhere.
⭐ AP Tested — What It DIDN'T Do
Specifically exempted: Tennessee (largely under Union control), parts of Louisiana including New Orleans, and the 48 counties of western Virginia that would become West Virginia. The border states — Missouri, Kentucky, Maryland, Delaware — which had not seceded and remained in the Union, were also entirely exempt. Enslaved people in these areas were not freed by the Proclamation.
Specific Exemptions
Lincoln couldn't afford to push the border states toward the Confederacy by threatening their enslaved labor force. Political calculation shaped the document as much as principle. The exemptions were not hypocrisy — they were the price of keeping the Union together while waging the war.
Political Calculation
IV. Political and Historical Significance
The Emancipation Proclamation transformed the Civil War from a war to preserve the Union into a war to end slavery — whether Lincoln intended it that way or not. Frederick Douglass, initially skeptical, recognized its power: "It is not a proclamation of 'liberty throughout all the land, unto all the inhabitants thereof,' such as we had hoped for, but it is a proclamation of freedom to the slaves of the rebels in arms." Lincoln himself knew the Proclamation's legal authority rested on shaky ground — which is why he pushed hard for the 13th Amendment in the final months of his life.
Douglass's Assessment
Frederick Douglass — himself a formerly enslaved man and the foremost Black leader of the era — initially criticized the Proclamation's limits. He later called it "the beginning of the end of slavery." His evolution mirrors the document itself: imperfect, politically constrained, but historically decisive.

AP Exam Connections
WHAT THE EXAM TESTS
  • Legal basis — commander-in-chief war powers, NOT moral declaration
  • What it freed: enslaved people in Confederate states only
  • What it did NOT free: enslaved people in Union border states
  • Why the 13th Amendment was still necessary
  • Its role in turning the war into a fight over slavery
  • Connection to Brown v. Board and civil rights tradition
COMMON MISTAKES
  • Saying it freed ALL enslaved people — only those in Confederate states
  • Saying it was a moral statement — Lincoln framed it as a WAR MEASURE
  • Forgetting it was an executive order, not a constitutional amendment
  • Thinking it alone ended slavery — the 13th Amendment (1865) did that
THE PROCLAMATION → 13TH AMENDMENT → 14TH AMENDMENT → BROWN V. BOARD
The Emancipation Proclamation (1863) began the legal dismantling of slavery but didn't finish it. The 13th Amendment (1865) permanently abolished slavery everywhere. The 14th Amendment (1868) guaranteed citizenship and equal protection for formerly enslaved people. Brown v. Board (1954) finally used the 14th Amendment to strike down the "separate but equal" doctrine that had allowed legal racial inequality for 58 years after the 14th Amendment was ratified. Understanding this chain — Proclamation → 13th → 14th → Brown — is essential for understanding the long arc of civil rights in America.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
The most tested fact about the Emancipation Proclamation is what it DIDN'T do — it didn't free enslaved people in Union border states and didn't permanently abolish slavery (that required the 13th Amendment). Know the legal basis (commander-in-chief war powers), the scope (Confederate states only), and why the 13th Amendment was still necessary.
