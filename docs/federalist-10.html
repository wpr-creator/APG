Historical Context — Read This First

It is November 1787. The Constitutional Convention has just ended. Nine of thirteen states need to ratify the new Constitution for it to take effect — and ratification is not guaranteed. New York, a large and powerful state, is deeply skeptical. Alexander Hamilton recruits James Madison and John Jay to write a series of essays defending the Constitution under the pen name "Publius." The result is 85 essays — the Federalist Papers — the most important work of political theory in American history.

Federalist No. 10 is Madison's answer to the central criticism of the new Constitution: that a republic — a system of representative government — can only work in a small territory where citizens know their representatives. Large republics, critics argued, inevitably become corrupt and tyrannical because representatives lose touch with the people they serve. Anti-Federalist "Brutus" had made this argument forcefully. Madison's response is counterintuitive and brilliant: larger is actually better.

Read this essay as argument, not as description. Madison is not explaining how American government works — he is trying to persuade skeptical New Yorkers to vote yes on ratification. Every sentence is doing rhetorical work.

📋 Federalist No. 10 is approximately 3,500 words. This page presents the complete required passages with full annotations. The essay's full text is available at the Avalon Project (avalon.law.yale.edu).
I. The Problem — What Are Factions?
Among the numerous advantages promised by a well-constructed Union, none deserves to be more accurately developed than its tendency to break and control the violence of faction. The friend of popular governments never finds himself so much alarmed for their character and fate, as when he contemplates their propensity to this dangerous vice.
Plain English
The biggest threat to democracy is factions — groups that put their own interests above the common good. Madison says this is the most important problem a well-designed government must solve.
Opening Move
By a faction, I understand a number of citizens, whether amounting to a majority or a minority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community.
Definition
A faction is any group — big or small — driven by shared self-interest that harms others. This includes political parties, interest groups, and voting blocs. Note: factions can be majorities too, not just minorities.
⭐ AP Tested — Know This Definition
There are two methods of curing the mischiefs of faction: the one, by removing its causes; the other, by controlling its effects. There are again two methods of removing the causes of faction: the one, by destroying the liberty which is essential to its existence; the other, by giving to every citizen the same opinions, the same passions, and the same interests.
The Setup
Madison lays out the logical options before ruling them all out. You can attack the cause of factions or the effect. But the two ways to attack the cause both destroy liberty — and that's worse than factions. He's boxing in his opponents before offering his solution.
Logical Structure
II. Two Solutions That Don't Work
It could never be more truly said than of the first remedy, that it was worse than the disease. Liberty is to faction what air is to fire, an aliment without which it instantly expires. But it could not be less folly to abolish liberty, which is essential to political life, because it nourishes faction, than it would be to wish the annihilation of air, which is essential to animal life, because it imparts to fire its destructive agency.
Key Metaphor
Liberty is to faction what air is to fire. You could eliminate fire by eliminating air — but then nothing lives. You can't eliminate factions by eliminating liberty — that cure is worse than the disease. This analogy is one of the most famous in American political writing.
⭐ AP Tested — Key Metaphor
The second expedient is as impracticable as the first would be unwise. As long as the reason of man continues fallible, and he is at liberty to exercise it, different opinions will be formed. As long as the connection subsists between his reason and his self-love, his opinions and his passions will have a reciprocal influence on each other; and the former will be objects to which the latter will attach themselves.
Plain English
You can't give everyone the same opinions because people are fallible and self-interested by nature. As long as humans are free to think, they'll think differently and put their own interests first. Forcing uniform opinion requires tyranny.
Human Nature Argument
The latent causes of faction are thus sown in the nature of man; and we see them everywhere brought into different degrees of activity, according to the different circumstances of civil society. The most common and durable source of factions has been the various and unequal distribution of property. Those who hold and those who are without property have ever formed distinct interests in society.
Root Cause
Economic inequality is the most persistent source of factions — the haves vs. the have-nots. Madison is being remarkably candid here: class conflict is built into society. This connects to modern debates about money in politics, interest groups, and economic policy.
⭐ AP Tested — Root Cause of Factions
III. The Solution — A Republic, Not a Democracy
The two great points of difference between a democracy and a republic are: first, the delegation of the government, in the latter, to a small number of citizens elected by the rest; secondly, the greater number of citizens, and greater sphere of country, over which the latter may be extended.
Key Distinction
Democracy = direct participation by all citizens. Republic = elected representatives making decisions on behalf of citizens. Madison argues a republic is better because representatives can filter out passionate, factional impulses and govern in the public interest.
⭐ AP Tested — Democracy vs. Republic
The effect of the first difference is, on the one hand, to refine and enlarge the public views, by passing them through the medium of a chosen body of citizens, whose wisdom may best discern the true interest of their country, and whose patriotism and love of justice will be least likely to sacrifice it to temporary or partial considerations.
The Filter Argument
Elected representatives "refine and enlarge" public opinion — they filter out the passion and self-interest of factions and govern more wisely than the mob would directly. This is Madison's argument for representative democracy over direct democracy.
Republicanism
IV. Why a Large Republic is Safer
Extend the sphere, and you take in a greater variety of parties and interests; you make it less probable that a majority of the whole will have a common motive to invade the rights of other citizens; or if such a common motive exists, it will be more difficult for all who feel it to discover their own strength, and to act in unison with each other.
The Core Argument
Larger republic = more factions = no single faction can dominate. In a small community one group can take over. In a large nation with hundreds of competing interests, they cancel each other out. This is the origin of American pluralism — the idea that democracy functions through competing interest groups.
⭐ AP Tested — Central Argument
Hence, it clearly appears, that the same advantage which a republic has over a democracy, in controlling the effects of faction, is enjoyed by a large over a small republic — is enjoyed by the Union over the States composing it.
The Conclusion
Madison lands his key point: the national Union is better than individual states at controlling faction for exactly the same reason that a republic is better than direct democracy. Size is a feature, not a bug. This is his answer to the Anti-Federalists who said large republics can't work.

CONNECTION TO BRUTUS NO. 1
Federalist 10 and Brutus No. 1 are a direct debate. Brutus argues that large republics inevitably lose touch with the people and are captured by elites — exactly what Madison claims a large republic prevents. On the AP exam, you may be asked to compare these two documents. Know both arguments cold: Madison says large republic = many factions = no one dominates. Brutus says large republic = distant government = elite control.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Federalist 10 is the most frequently cited foundational document on the AP exam. Know Madison's definition of faction, his two failed solutions, the democracy vs. republic distinction, and the large republic argument. The "extend the sphere" passage is the one you need to be able to explain in your own words.
