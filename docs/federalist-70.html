Federalist No. 70

Historical Context — Read This First

By March 1788, the Constitution was being debated intensely in New York — one of the most skeptical states. Antifederalists feared the new presidency would become an elected monarchy: one man with enormous power, no council to check him, serving renewable four-year terms. Hamilton wrote Federalist 70 to defend the structural choice of a single, energetic executive directly against this fear.

Read this as Hamilton's constitutional theory of the presidency: why unity matters, why energy is a virtue not a danger, and how the accountability mechanisms (elections, impeachment, advice and consent) make executive power safe for a republic. Every debate about presidential power — from Lincoln's war powers to executive orders to modern debates about the 'unitary executive' — plays out in the framework Hamilton built here.

I. Energy in the Executive — Hamilton's Core Claim
"Energy in the executive is a leading character in the definition of good government." Hamilton argues that a vigorous, decisive executive is not a threat to liberty — it IS essential to liberty. Without energy, government cannot defend against foreign attack, enforce laws against domestic faction, protect property rights, or secure civil liberties.
Energy = Good Government
Hamilton directly confronts the Antifederalist fear of executive tyranny. His answer: weak executives have historically produced anarchy or been captured by factions, both of which destroy liberty more surely than a strong executive. The British King's abuse of power came from a hereditary monarch with no accountability — not from executive energy itself.
⭐ AP Tested
Hamilton identifies four ingredients of executive energy: unity, duration, adequate provision, and competent powers. And two ingredients of executive safety: due dependence on the people and due responsibility. Both energy and accountability are required — neither alone is sufficient.
The Six Ingredients
This framework is testable: energy (unity, duration, adequate provision, competent powers) + safety (dependence on people, responsibility). On the AP exam, if asked what makes a strong executive, cite Hamilton's framework from Federalist 70.
II. Unity — One President, Not a Council
"That unity is conducive to energy will not be disputed. Decision, activity, secrecy, and dispatch will generally characterize the proceedings of one man in a much more eminent degree than the proceedings of any greater number." Hamilton defends a single executive against proposals for an executive council or plural presidency.
One vs. Many
Some Antifederalists proposed a plural executive — three co-presidents, or a council that must approve presidential actions. Hamilton argues this destroys accountability: when things go wrong, who is responsible? A plural executive diffuses blame. A single president is visibly, personally accountable for every decision.
⭐ Key Concept
A council of executives, Hamilton argues, would produce "habitual feebleness and dilatoriness" — constant disagreement, paralysis, and delay. Worse, it would conceal responsibility: "it often becomes impossible, amidst mutual accusations, to determine on whom the blame or the punishment of a pernicious measure... ought really to fall."
The Accountability Problem
This is Hamilton's most powerful argument: accountability requires a single identifiable decision-maker. When a council makes a bad decision, everyone points fingers. When one president makes a bad decision, the public knows exactly who to hold responsible — and can vote them out or impeach them.
⭐ AP Tested
III. Accountability — The Safety Counterbalance
Hamilton acknowledges the danger: a single energetic executive could abuse power. His answer is the system of checks — the Senate confirms appointments, Congress controls the purse, and most importantly, the president is elected (not hereditary), serves a limited term, and is subject to impeachment for high crimes and misdemeanors.
Built-In Accountability
The difference between the British King and the American President, Hamilton argues, is accountability. The King is hereditary and permanent; the President is elected and removable. Energy without accountability = tyranny (the King). Accountability without energy = weak government. The Constitution gives both.
EK 2.1.A
The Senate's role in advice and consent — confirming appointments and ratifying treaties — is the primary check on executive energy in foreign affairs and personnel. In Federalist 70, Hamilton focuses on the executive acting alone: speed, secrecy, and dispatch in execution of laws.
Senate as Check
Federalist 70 and 76 pair together: 70 argues for executive energy and unity; 76 argues for Senate confirmation as the check on the worst appointment abuses. Together they define the constitutional balance between presidential power and legislative oversight.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Federalist 70 is tested on the AP exam as Hamilton's defense of the unitary executive. Know the key argument: unity (one president) produces energy (decision, secrecy, dispatch) and accountability (clear responsibility). FRQs may ask you to use Federalist 70 to explain why the Founders chose a single president instead of an executive council. Always pair energy (Federalist 70) with accountability — Hamilton wanted both, not just one.

Common Mistakes
✗Saying Hamilton wanted an all-powerful president — he explicitly pairs energy WITH accountability mechanisms.
✗Forgetting the four ingredients of energy: unity, duration, adequate provision, competent powers.
✗Confusing Federalist 70 (executive energy/unity) with Federalist 78 (judicial review) — related themes, different arguments.
