Historical Context — Read This First

Antifederalists were deeply suspicious of the new federal judiciary — unelected judges with life tenure who could strike down laws passed by elected legislatures. Was this judicial tyranny dressed up in robes? Hamilton wrote Federalist 78 to defend the judicial branch as the safest, least powerful branch — and to argue that judicial independence through life tenure was essential precisely because courts must protect constitutional rights against political majorities.

This is the document that laid the intellectual foundation for Marbury v. Madison (1803) and the entire tradition of American judicial review. Read it as Hamilton's answer to a question that is still debated today: should unelected judges be able to override democratically elected legislatures?

I. The Least Dangerous Branch
"Whoever attentively considers the different departments of power must perceive, that, in a government in which they are separated from each other, the judiciary, from the nature of its functions, will always be the least dangerous to the political rights of the Constitution." The executive has the sword; the legislature has the purse. The judiciary has neither — only judgment.
No Force, No Will
Hamilton is preemptively answering the Antifederalist fear that an unelected federal judiciary would become a judicial oligarchy. His answer: courts have no army and no budget. They depend entirely on the executive to enforce their rulings. They can declare a law void — but they cannot force anyone to comply. The power is real but inherently limited.
⭐ AP Tested
"The judiciary... has no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society; and can take no active resolution whatever. It may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment."
Force, Will, Judgment
Hamilton's three-way distinction is essential for the AP exam: executive = force (enforces laws), legislature = will (makes laws expressing popular will), judiciary = judgment (applies law to specific cases). Courts don't make law or enforce it — they only interpret it. This is why Hamilton argues judges need protection from political pressure.
⭐ Key Framework
II. Judicial Review — Courts Can Void Acts of Congress
"No legislative act... contrary to the Constitution, can be valid... The interpretation of the laws is the proper and peculiar province of the courts." Hamilton anticipates judicial review — the power of courts to strike down laws that violate the Constitution — fifteen years before Marbury v. Madison formally established it.
Anticipating Marbury
The Constitution never explicitly grants courts the power to strike down acts of Congress. Hamilton argues it is implied: the Constitution is the supreme law; courts interpret law; therefore courts must determine whether ordinary laws conform to the supreme law. This logic became the foundation of Marbury v. Madison (1803).
⭐ Connects to Marbury
Where the will of the legislature conflicts with the will of the people expressed in the Constitution, "the judges ought to be governed by the latter rather than the former." Courts exist to enforce the Constitution against temporary legislative majorities — protecting the long-term will of the people against short-term passions.
Courts vs. Majorities
This is Hamilton's most important political theory claim: judicial review protects constitutional rights from majoritarian override. A temporary majority in Congress might pass an unconstitutional law in a moment of passion or interest. Courts, insulated from politics, enforce the constitutional limits the people themselves established. Judges are the people's agents against their own representatives.
⭐ AP Tested
III. Life Tenure — Protecting Independence
"The standard of good behavior for the continuance in office of the judicial magistracy is certainly one of the most valuable of the modern improvements in the practice of government." Hamilton defends Article III's guarantee that federal judges hold office "during good behavior" — effectively life tenure unless impeached.
Good Behavior = Life Tenure
The Constitution guarantees federal judges their positions unless removed through impeachment. Hamilton argues this is essential to judicial independence: a judge who fears losing their position will decide cases to please whoever controls their appointment — the president, the Senate, or the public. Life tenure insulates judges from political pressure.
⭐ AP Tested
"That inflexible and uniform adherence to the rights of the Constitution, and of individuals, which we perceive to be indispensable in the courts of justice, can certainly not be expected from judges who hold their offices by a temporary commission." Temporary judges become political actors. Life-tenured judges can follow the law wherever it leads.
Independence Requires Permanence
This is Hamilton's core argument: independence (from political pressure) requires permanence (no need to please the appointing authority to keep your job). The tradeoff is reduced direct accountability — but Hamilton argues this is the right tradeoff for the branch that must protect constitutional rights against majorities.
Structural Argument
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Federalist 78 is paired with Marbury v. Madison on the AP exam — Hamilton anticipates judicial review; Marshall establishes it. Know the three key arguments: (1) judiciary is least dangerous (no force or will, only judgment), (2) courts must be able to strike down unconstitutional laws (judicial review), (3) life tenure ensures independence from political pressure. FRQs about judicial independence or the power of the courts → cite Federalist 78.

Common Mistakes
✗Saying Hamilton opposed judicial review — he explicitly argues courts MUST be able to void unconstitutional laws.
✗Forgetting life tenure applies only to FEDERAL judges — state judges are often elected to fixed terms.
✗Confusing Federalist 78 (courts) with Federalist 70 (executive) — both are by Hamilton but address different branches.
