Schenck v. United States
249 U.S. 47 · March 3, 1919 · Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes
Unit 3 1st Amendment Free Speech ⭐ Frequently Tested
📋
BACKGROUND

World War I, 1917. Congress passed the Espionage Act making it a crime to obstruct military recruitment. Charles Schenck, general secretary of the Socialist Party, mailed nearly 15,000 leaflets to men drafted into the military, urging them to resist conscription — calling the draft a form of "despotism" and invoking the 13th Amendment's prohibition on involuntary servitude. He was convicted under the Espionage Act.

Schenck argued his leaflets were protected by the First Amendment. The Supreme Court unanimously disagreed. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote the opinion — and in doing so created the most famous and contested test in First Amendment history.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Does the First Amendment protect speech that attempts to obstruct military recruitment during wartime?
⚖️
THE RULING
✗ Speech NOT protected — Unanimous (9-0)
Unanimous · Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes
"The most stringent protection of free speech would not protect a man in falsely shouting fire in a theatre and causing a panic... The question in every case is whether the words are used in such circumstances and are of such a nature as to create a clear and present danger."
Holmes held that speech creating a "clear and present danger" of producing evils that Congress has the right to prevent is not constitutionally protected. In wartime, anti-draft leaflets pose exactly that danger. The famous "shouting fire in a crowded theatre" analogy was Holmes's way of illustrating that context matters — the same words can be protected in peacetime and criminal in wartime. The First Amendment is not absolute.
📐
TEST ESTABLISHED
The "Clear and Present Danger" Test
Speech may be restricted when it creates a clear and present danger of producing a substantive evil that the government has a right to prevent. The test asks: (1) Is the danger clear — not merely possible but probable? (2) Is it present — imminent, not distant? (3) Is it a danger to something the government can legitimately protect against? This test has since been replaced by the Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969) "imminent lawless action" standard, which is more protective of speech. But Schenck established the foundational principle that the 1st Amendment is not absolute.
🔗
LEGACY & LATER CASES
  • Abrams v. U.S.
    1919    Holmes dissented — he felt the majority applied Schenck too broadly to punish merely offensive speech. His dissent is one of the most important free speech writings in history: "the ultimate good desired is better reached by free trade in ideas."
  • Brandenburg v. Ohio
    1969    Largely replaced Schenck. The Court held government can only restrict speech that is directed to inciting imminent lawless action AND is likely to produce such action. Higher protection than Schenck's "clear and present danger."
  • Shouting Fire
    Misquote    Holmes actually said "falsely shouting fire in a theatre" — the word "falsely" matters enormously. Truly shouting fire when there is one is protected. The misquote is used constantly to justify speech restrictions Holmes never intended to allow.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Schenck is tested as the origin of speech restriction doctrine — know the clear and present danger test AND that Brandenburg (1969) largely replaced it with a stricter standard. On the AP exam, Schenck almost always appears in context of comparing earlier vs. later speech protection. Know that Schenck RESTRICTED speech — it ruled AGAINST the speaker.

Common Mistakes
✗Thinking Schenck PROTECTED speech — it ruled AGAINST Schenck. The government won.
✗Using the shouting fire analogy without the word 'falsely' — Holmes's full quote includes it.
✗Thinking clear and present danger is still the standard — Brandenburg (1969) raised the bar.
