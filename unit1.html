Unit 1: Foundations of
American Democracy
The Founders argued about power — how much government should have, who should hold it, and what happens when it's abused. Every document in this unit is either a warning about tyranny or a design for preventing it. They were all writing for their lives.

Key Concepts
7 concepts
1
Natural Rights & Social Contract
⌄
Enlightenment thinkers — especially John Locke — argued that people are born with natural rights (life, liberty, property) that no government can legitimately take away. Government exists by a "social contract": people give up some freedom in exchange for protection. If the government breaks the contract by violating rights, the people have the right to alter or abolish it. Jefferson built the Declaration of Independence on this framework. It is the philosophical DNA of the American founding.
2
The Articles of Confederation — Why They Failed
⌄
America's first constitution gave almost all power to the states. Congress could not tax, could not raise an army without state cooperation, could not regulate commerce between states, and needed unanimous consent to amend anything. Shays' Rebellion (1786) — a debt crisis in Massachusetts where the state government nearly collapsed — convinced leaders that the Articles were fatally weak. The Constitutional Convention (1787) was called to fix this. Instead, the delegates scrapped the Articles entirely and wrote a new constitution.
3
Constitutional Compromises
⌄
The Constitution was not inevitable — it required four major compromises. The Great Compromise created a bicameral legislature: the House (population-based) satisfied large states, the Senate (equal representation) satisfied small states. The Three-Fifths Compromise counted enslaved people as 3/5 of a person for representation — a moral catastrophe that empowered slaveholding states. The Commerce and Slave Trade Compromise prevented Congress from banning the slave trade until 1808. The Electoral College was created as a compromise between direct popular election and congressional selection of the president.
4
Federalism
⌄
Federalism divides power between the national government and state governments, each with its own sphere of authority. The Constitution gives the federal government enumerated powers (listed in Article I, Section 8) and implied powers (from the Necessary and Proper Clause). The 10th Amendment reserves all other powers to the states. Dual federalism (layer cake) kept the spheres separate; cooperative federalism (marble cake) has them intertwined since the New Deal. The Supremacy Clause establishes that when federal and state law conflict, federal law wins.
5
Separation of Powers & Checks and Balances
⌄
The Constitution divides power among three branches — legislative (Congress), executive (President), and judicial (courts) — so no single branch can dominate. But separation alone isn't enough; each branch also has tools to limit the others. Congress can override a presidential veto, the President nominates judges and can veto legislation, the courts can strike down laws as unconstitutional. Madison called this "ambition must be made to counteract ambition" — the system relies on the self-interest of each branch to prevent another from overreaching. This is why the system sometimes appears gridlocked: it's designed to make action difficult.
6
Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist Debate
⌄
Ratification wasn't guaranteed. Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) argued in the Federalist Papers that a strong central government was necessary to prevent the chaos of the Articles era. A large republic, Madison argued in Federalist 10, would actually protect liberty better than a small one — more factions would cancel each other out. Anti-Federalists (writing as "Brutus," "Federal Farmer") feared the new government would destroy states' rights and individual liberty. They demanded a Bill of Rights as the price of ratification. Without that concession, the Constitution might not have been ratified. The Anti-Federalists lost the vote but won the argument about the Bill of Rights.
7
American Political Values & Culture
⌄
American political culture rests on a set of core values that most Americans broadly share despite political disagreements: liberty (freedom from government interference), equality (equal treatment under law and equal political participation), democracy (government by consent of the governed), individualism (personal responsibility and self-reliance), and rule of law (no one is above the law, including the government). These values coexist in tension — liberty and equality sometimes conflict — and Americans disagree about how to balance them. Understanding this tension is essential for AP Government.
Required Foundational Documents
6 documents
🦅
Declaration of Independence
Thomas Jefferson · July 4, 1776
The founding argument for natural rights and self-governance. Jefferson built the case for revolution on Locke's social contract theory. But read it as a legal document, not a poem — it lists 27 specific grievances against King George III, each one a violation of the social contract. The famous second paragraph is the philosophy; the grievance list is the proof.

Articles of Confederation
Continental Congress · Ratified 1781
America's first constitution — and a cautionary tale. The Articles created a national government so weak it could barely function: no power to tax, no power to regulate commerce, no executive, no national court system. On the AP exam, you need to know what the Articles could NOT do as much as what they could. The Constitution was written in reaction to these failures.

⚖️
U.S. Constitution
Constitutional Convention · Ratified 1788
The operating manual for American government. The required portions for AP include the Preamble, Article I (Congress), Article II (President), Article III (Courts), the Supremacy Clause, the Necessary and Proper Clause, and key amendments. You do not need to memorize the whole document — but you need to know every clause that has generated a landmark case.

✍️
Federalist No. 10
James Madison · November 22, 1787
Madison's most important essay. He argues that factions — groups driven by self-interest at the expense of others — are inevitable in a free society. The solution isn't eliminating factions (that would require eliminating liberty) but controlling their effects. A large republic with many competing factions is safer than a small one where a single faction can dominate. This argument is the foundation for pluralism in American politics.
Read Full Document with Annotations →
✍️
Federalist No. 51
James Madison · February 6, 1788
Madison's defense of separation of powers and checks and balances. "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Each branch is given the tools to resist the others — and each branch's members have personal incentives to use those tools. The system doesn't depend on virtue; it's designed to work even when people in power act selfishly. The most important essay for understanding how the Constitution actually works.
Read Full Document with Annotations →
⚡
Brutus No. 1
Robert Yates (attributed) · October 18, 1787
The most important Anti-Federalist essay — and the best counterargument to Madison. "Brutus" warns that the Necessary and Proper Clause and Supremacy Clause give the federal government unlimited power to destroy the states. A large republic, he argues, cannot represent the people — it's too big, too distant, and too easily captured by elites. Read this alongside Federalist 10 and 51: together they frame the debate that still shapes American politics.

Key Amendments
Bill of Rights
1st
Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition
The most litigated amendment. Five distinct freedoms in one sentence. All are limited — none are absolute.
2nd
Right to Bear Arms
Individual right per Heller (2008), applied to states via McDonald (2010). Most politically contested right in current law.
4th
Search and Seizure
Unreasonable searches prohibited; warrants require probable cause. Exclusionary rule (Mapp v. Ohio) flows from this.
5th
Self-Incrimination, Due Process
Can't be forced to testify against yourself. Can't be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process. Foundation of Miranda.
6th
Right to Counsel & Fair Trial
Speedy trial, impartial jury, right to an attorney. Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) applied this to the states.
10th
Reserved Powers
Powers not given to the federal government are reserved to the states or the people. The constitutional foundation of states' rights arguments.

COMMON MISTAKES
THE BIG PICTURE
  • The specific weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation — know at least five
  • The Great Compromise — why both chambers exist and how they differ
  • Madison's argument in Federalist 10 — factions, large republic, pluralism
  • Madison's argument in Federalist 51 — separation of powers, ambition counteracting ambition
  • Brutus No. 1's counterarguments — Necessary and Proper Clause, Supremacy Clause concerns
  • The difference between enumerated, implied, reserved, and concurrent powers
  • Dual federalism vs. cooperative federalism — and which era each belongs to
  • Confusing Federalists (pro-Constitution) with federalism (the system of divided power) — they're related but different
  • Thinking the Declaration of Independence is a legal document — it has no legal force; the Constitution is the law
  • Forgetting that the Three-Fifths Compromise applied to enslaved people, not Native Americans or free Black people
  • Saying Madison wrote Federalist 51 alone — he likely wrote it, but some scholars attribute it to Hamilton. On the AP exam, just know the argument
  • Confusing the Necessary and Proper Clause (implied powers) with the Supremacy Clause (federal law wins over state law) — they do different things
Unit 1 is about one central tension: how do you create a government strong enough to function but not so strong it becomes tyrannical? The Founders had just fought a revolution against an overpowering central authority. They were terrified of concentrating power — in a king, in a majority, in any single faction. Every design choice in the Constitution is a response to that fear. The separation of powers, federalism, checks and balances, the Bill of Rights — all of it is a system for preventing any one group from controlling everything. Understand that fear, and you understand the whole unit.
