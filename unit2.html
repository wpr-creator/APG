Unit 2: Interactions Among
Branches of Government
Three branches. One Constitution. Constant tension. Congress makes the laws, the President executes them, the courts interpret them — and each branch has tools to block the other two. Understanding how these institutions check each other is the core of Unit 2.

Key Concepts
7 concepts
1
Separation of Powers in Practice
⌄
The Constitution divides government into three branches — legislative, executive, judicial — each with distinct functions and powers. Congress makes law, the President executes law, the courts interpret law. But in practice these functions overlap constantly: the President issues executive orders that look like law, agencies write regulations that function as law, and courts sometimes look like they're making policy. The clean separation on paper is messier in reality, which is exactly why checks and balances are necessary.
2
Checks and Balances
⌄
Each branch has specific constitutional tools to limit the other two. Congress can override a presidential veto (2/3 majority), impeach and remove the President and federal judges, and refuse to confirm appointments or ratify treaties. The President can veto legislation, nominate federal judges, and use executive orders. Courts can declare laws and executive actions unconstitutional. These mechanisms mean no single branch can act unchecked — but they also create gridlock when the branches are controlled by opposing parties.
3
Congressional Powers — Enumerated and Implied
⌄
Article I, Section 8 lists Congress's enumerated (express) powers: taxation, borrowing, commerce regulation, declaring war, raising armies, coining money, and more. The Necessary and Proper Clause then expands these by allowing Congress to "make all Laws... necessary and proper" to carry out the enumerated powers — the source of implied powers. McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) established that implied powers are broad: Congress doesn't need an explicit grant to do something, just a reasonable connection to an enumerated power.
4
Presidential Powers — Formal and Informal
⌄
Formal presidential powers come directly from Article II: commander-in-chief, treaty negotiation, appointments, the veto, pardons, and State of the Union. Informal powers have developed over time through practice and political evolution: executive orders, executive agreements (bypassing the Senate's treaty power), signing statements, and the bully pulpit — using the presidency's platform to influence public opinion and pressure Congress. The modern presidency exercises far more power than the Constitution's text suggests.
5
Judicial Review
⌄
Judicial review is the power of courts to strike down laws and executive actions that violate the Constitution. It is not in the Constitution — Chief Justice John Marshall established it in Marbury v. Madison (1803). Marshall's logic: the Constitution is supreme law; courts decide what the law means; therefore courts must be able to void laws that conflict with the Constitution. Without judicial review, the Constitution would be unenforceable against Congress and the President. Hamilton anticipated this power in Federalist 78.
6
The Federal Bureaucracy
⌄
The bureaucracy — the Cabinet departments, independent agencies, and regulatory bodies that actually implement federal law — is not mentioned in the Constitution but has become the fourth branch in practice. Congress creates agencies and funds them; the President appoints agency heads and directs policy; courts review agency decisions. Bureaucrats exercise enormous discretionary power through rulemaking, enforcement decisions, and policy interpretation. Iron triangles — the tight relationships between congressional committees, executive agencies, and interest groups — often determine policy outcomes more than elected officials do.
7
Federalism and the Commerce Clause
⌄
The Commerce Clause (Article I, Sec. 8) gives Congress power to regulate commerce "among the several States." This has been the constitutional basis for most federal economic regulation since the New Deal. The Court interpreted it broadly until U.S. v. Lopez (1995) — the first time in 60 years the Court struck down a federal law for exceeding the Commerce Clause. Lopez established limits: Congress can regulate economic activity that substantially affects interstate commerce, but not non-economic activity just because it might indirectly affect commerce.

  • Execute federal law

  • Issue executive orders
  • Pardon federal offenses

SUPREME COURT + FEDERAL COURTS
  • Interpret federal law
  • Judicial review (Marbury)
  • Rule on constitutional questions
  • Strike down laws + exec. actions
  • Life tenure (good behavior)
  • Original + appellate jurisdiction
How Each Branch Checks the Others
Congress checks...
→ President: veto override, impeachment, power of the purse, war powers
→ Courts: jurisdiction, impeachment, constitutional amendments, court-packing (political)
President checks...
→ Congress: veto, special sessions, bully pulpit
→ Courts: nominates all federal judges, sets DOJ enforcement priorities
Courts check...
→ Congress: strike down laws as unconstitutional
→ President: strike down executive orders, limit emergency powers
Required SCOTUS Cases
3 cases
1803
Judicial Review
Marbury v. Madison
Does the Supreme Court have the power to void acts of Congress that conflict with the Constitution?
Yes — Marshall established judicial review. The Constitution is supreme law; courts interpret law; therefore courts must be able to void unconstitutional legislation. The most important case in American legal history.
→
1819
Implied Powers
McCulloch v. Maryland
Can Congress charter a national bank, and can states tax it?
Yes Congress can (implied powers via N&P Clause); No states cannot tax it (Supremacy Clause). Established that federal power is broad and supreme over conflicting state action.
→
1995
Commerce Clause
United States v. Lopez
Does Congress have Commerce Clause authority to ban guns near schools?
No — the Gun-Free School Zones Act exceeded congressional power. Carrying a gun near a school is not economic activity substantially affecting interstate commerce. First Commerce Clause limit in 60 years.
→
Required Foundational Documents
3 documents
⚡
Federalist No. 70
Alexander Hamilton · March 15, 1788
Hamilton's defense of a single, energetic executive. He argues that "energy in the executive is a leading character in the definition of good government." A committee-style executive (as some proposed) would be weak, slow, and prone to internal conflict. One President means clear accountability and decisive action.

🏛️
Federalist No. 78
Alexander Hamilton · May 28, 1788
Hamilton's case for an independent judiciary with life tenure and the power of judicial review. Courts are the "least dangerous branch" — they have neither force nor will, only judgment. But that judgment must be independent of political pressure to protect constitutional rights against temporary majorities.

COMMON MISTAKES
THE BIG PICTURE
  • Judicial review — what it is, where it came from (Marbury), why Hamilton anticipated it in Fed. 78
  • Implied powers — the N&P Clause + McCulloch + the logic of "let the end be legitimate"
  • Commerce Clause limits — Lopez established that non-economic activity isn't covered
  • Formal vs. informal presidential powers — know specific examples of each
  • The appointment and confirmation process — President nominates, Senate confirms
  • Iron triangles — congressional committees + agencies + interest groups
  • Hamilton's argument for an energetic executive in Federalist 70
  • Saying judicial review is in the Constitution — it is NOT; Marshall created it in Marbury
  • Confusing McCulloch (implied powers + supremacy) with Lopez (Commerce Clause limits) — they cut in opposite directions
  • Thinking the President can make treaties alone — requires 2/3 Senate ratification
  • Forgetting that executive orders can be overturned by courts or reversed by the next President
  • Saying Congress controls the bureaucracy — both Congress AND the President have authority over agencies, which creates tension
Unit 2 is about institutional design under pressure. The Constitution created a structure, but that structure has been tested, stretched, and reinterpreted constantly. The President has more power than Article II says. Congress has less than it seems. The bureaucracy wasn't imagined by the Founders but now employs millions. Courts have the power of judicial review despite the Constitution never saying so. The question isn't just what the Constitution says — it's what each branch has been able to claim and hold over 230 years of political conflict.
