Civil Liberties & Civil Rights

Where does the government's power end and your rights begin? Civil liberties protect individuals from government. Civil rights protect individuals from discrimination. The 14th Amendment is the engine that drives this entire unit.

Key Concepts
6 concepts
1
Civil Liberties vs. Civil Rights
⌄
Civil liberties are protections FROM government — freedom of speech, freedom from unreasonable searches. Civil rights are protections AGAINST discrimination — the right to be treated equally regardless of race, gender, or religion. Students consistently confuse these. Civil liberties = what the government cannot do to you. Civil rights = the right to be treated the same as everyone else.
2
Selective Incorporation
⌄
The Bill of Rights originally only applied to the federal government — states could do whatever they wanted. The 14th Amendment (1868) changed this by making states follow due process and equal protection. Through selective incorporation, the Supreme Court has applied most of the Bill of Rights to the states one case at a time. Mapp v. Ohio, Gideon v. Wainwright, and Miranda v. Arizona are all examples of this process at work.
3
The First Amendment
⌄
Congress cannot establish an official religion (Establishment Clause), cannot prevent you from practicing your religion (Free Exercise Clause), cannot restrict speech or press, and cannot stop peaceful assembly or petitioning. But none of these are absolute. The Court has carved out categories of unprotected speech (true threats, incitement, obscenity) and created tests — like the "clear and present danger" test from Schenck — to decide when restrictions are constitutional.
4
Rights of the Accused
⌄
The 4th, 5th, 6th, and 8th Amendments protect people accused of crimes. The 4th requires warrants and prohibits unreasonable searches (Mapp v. Ohio applied this to states). The 5th protects against self-incrimination (Miranda). The 6th guarantees the right to counsel (Gideon). These rights reflect the Founders' deep distrust of government power — especially the power to imprison and punish citizens.
5
Equal Protection
⌄
The 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause says no state can deny any person equal protection of the laws. Courts use different levels of scrutiny: strict scrutiny (race, national origin) requires a compelling government interest; intermediate scrutiny (gender) requires an important interest; rational basis requires just a legitimate reason. Brown v. Board demolished the "separate but equal" doctrine that had allowed racial segregation for 58 years after Plessy v. Ferguson.
6
Privacy Rights
⌄
Privacy is never mentioned in the Constitution — but the Court has found it implied in the "penumbras and emanations" of the Bill of Rights and in the 14th Amendment's liberty clause. Roe v. Wade (1973) built on this foundation to find a right to abortion. Though Roe was overturned in Dobbs v. Jackson (2022), the underlying privacy doctrine remains important for understanding how the Court interprets unenumerated rights — rights not explicitly listed in the Constitution.
Required SCOTUS Cases
8 cases
1919
1st Amend.
Schenck v. United States
Can government restrict speech creating a "clear and present danger"?
Yes — Holmes established the clear and present danger test. Anti-draft leaflets during WWI were not protected.
→
1962
Est. Clause
Engel v. Vitale
Does state-sponsored prayer in public schools violate the Establishment Clause?
Yes — even a voluntary, non-denominational prayer written by the state crosses the constitutional line.
→
1963
6th Amend.
Gideon v. Wainwright
Must states provide a lawyer to felony defendants who can't afford one?
Yes — Gideon's handwritten appeal won a unanimous ruling. The right to counsel is fundamental to a fair trial.
→
1969
1st Amend.
Tinker v. Des Moines
Do students shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate?
No — schools can only restrict student speech causing "substantial disruption." Silent armbands don't meet that standard.
→
1971
1st Amend.
New York Times v. United States
Can government stop a newspaper from publishing the Pentagon Papers?
No — prior restraint is the most serious First Amendment violation. The government couldn't meet the extraordinarily heavy burden required.
→
1972
Free Exercise
Wisconsin v. Yoder
Can the state compel Amish families to keep children in school past 8th grade?
No — Wisconsin's compulsory education law violated the Free Exercise Clause. The state's interest didn't outweigh centuries-old religious practice.
→
1954
Eq. Protection
Brown v. Board of Education
Does racial segregation in public schools violate the Equal Protection Clause?
Yes — "Separate but equal is inherently unequal." Unanimous. Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896). Most important civil rights ruling in American history.
→
2010
2nd & 14th
McDonald v. City of Chicago
Does the 2nd Amendment apply to state and local governments?
Yes — the most recent major selective incorporation case. The 2nd Amendment right to keep and bear arms applies to all levels of government.

Emancipation Proclamation
Abraham Lincoln · January 1, 1863
Issued under Lincoln's commander-in-chief authority, it declared enslaved people in Confederate states free — but not in Union border states. A deliberate political choice that reframed the Civil War and set the stage for the 13th Amendment. Know what it did AND what it didn't do.

The Gettysburg Address
Abraham Lincoln · November 19, 1863
Two minutes, 272 words, delivered at the dedication of a Civil War cemetery. Lincoln reframes the war as a test of whether a nation "conceived in liberty" and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal can endure — quietly redefining the Declaration of Independence, not the Constitution, as the nation's founding promise of equality.

Letter from Birmingham Jail
Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. · April 16, 1963
Written from a jail cell in response to white clergy who called his protests "unwise and untimely." One of the most precisely argued defenses of civil disobedience ever written. Read it as argument — King distinguishes just from unjust laws and responds to every counterargument against the movement.

Key Amendments
8 amendments
1st
Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly
The most litigated amendment. Establishment Clause (no state religion), Free Exercise Clause, free speech, free press, peaceful assembly. None are absolute — the Court decides limits.
2nd
Right to Bear Arms
Individual right per Heller (2008). Applied to states via McDonald (2010). One of the most politically contested rights in current law.
4th
Search and Seizure
Unreasonable searches prohibited; warrants require probable cause. The exclusionary rule (Mapp v. Ohio) means illegal evidence can't be used in court.
5th
Self-Incrimination & Due Process
Can't be forced to testify against yourself (Miranda), can't be tried twice for the same crime, can't be deprived of life/liberty/property without due process.
6th
Right to Counsel & Fair Trial
Speedy trial, impartial jury, right to confront witnesses, right to an attorney. Gideon (1963) applied the right to counsel to the states.
8th
Cruel and Unusual Punishment
Excessive bail, fines, and cruel/unusual punishment prohibited. Applies to death penalty debates, juvenile sentencing, and prison conditions.
13th
Abolition of Slavery
Slavery abolished except as criminal punishment. Made permanent what the Emancipation Proclamation only partially achieved. Ratified 1865.
14th
Citizenship, Due Process, Equal Protection ★
The engine of Unit 3. Every selective incorporation case runs through its Due Process Clause. Brown v. Board used its Equal Protection Clause. Know this amendment cold.

THE BIG PICTURE
  • Selective incorporation — how and why it works
  • The 14th Amendment's two key clauses (Due Process + Equal Protection)
  • The "substantial disruption" test from Tinker
  • Prior restraint and why it's almost never allowed
  • The difference between Establishment and Free Exercise clauses
  • Why Brown overturned Plessy — and what "inherently" means
  • Miranda warnings — what triggers them and what they require
  • Confusing civil liberties (FROM government) with civil rights (FROM discrimination)
  • Thinking the Bill of Rights automatically applies to states — it doesn't without incorporation
  • Saying Roe v. Wade is "still the law" — overturned in 2022 by Dobbs
  • Confusing Miranda (right to remain silent) with Gideon (right to a lawyer)
  • Forgetting Schenck's "clear and present danger" test was limiting speech, not protecting it
Unit 3 is about the ongoing negotiation between individual freedom and government authority. Every case involves a conflict: the government wants to do something (censor the press, search a house, maintain segregated schools) and an individual claims a constitutional right prevents it. The Court's answer almost always depends on which level of scrutiny applies and whether the government has a sufficiently important interest to justify the restriction. Learn the framework, not just the outcomes.
