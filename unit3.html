Unit 3: Civil Liberties & Civil Rights
This unit asks one fundamental question: where does the government's power end and your rights begin? Civil liberties protect individuals from government intrusion. Civil rights protect individuals from discrimination. The 14th Amendment connects almost everything here — it is the engine that drives this entire unit.

Key Concepts
6 concepts
Civil Liberties vs. Civil Rights
Civil liberties are protections FROM government — freedom of speech, freedom from unreasonable searches. Civil rights are protections AGAINST discrimination — the right to be treated equally regardless of race, gender, or religion. Students consistently confuse these. Civil liberties = what the government cannot do to you. Civil rights = the right to be treated the same as everyone else.
Selective Incorporation
The Bill of Rights originally only applied to the federal government — states could do whatever they wanted. The 14th Amendment (1868) changed this by making states follow due process and equal protection. Through selective incorporation, the Supreme Court has applied most of the Bill of Rights to the states one case at a time. Mapp v. Ohio, Gideon v. Wainwright, and Miranda v. Arizona are all examples of this process.
The First Amendment
Congress cannot establish an official religion (Establishment Clause), cannot prevent you from practicing your religion (Free Exercise Clause), cannot restrict speech or press, and cannot stop peaceful assembly or petitioning. But none of these are absolute. The Court has carved out categories of unprotected speech (true threats, incitement, obscenity) and created tests — like the "clear and present danger" test from Schenck — to decide when restrictions are constitutional.
Rights of the Accused
The 4th, 5th, 6th, and 8th Amendments protect people accused of crimes. The 4th requires warrants and prohibits unreasonable searches (Mapp v. Ohio applied this to states). The 5th protects against self-incrimination (Miranda). The 6th guarantees the right to counsel (Gideon). These rights reflect the Founders' deep distrust of government power — especially the power to imprison and punish citizens.
Equal Protection
The 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause says no state can deny any person equal protection of the laws. Courts use different levels of scrutiny depending on the classification at issue: strict scrutiny (race, national origin) requires a compelling government interest; intermediate scrutiny (gender) requires an important interest; rational basis (most other classifications) just requires a legitimate reason. Brown v. Board demolished the "separate but equal" doctrine that had allowed racial segregation for 58 years.
Privacy Rights
Privacy is never mentioned in the Constitution — but the Court has found it implied in the "penumbras and emanations" of the Bill of Rights and in the 14th Amendment's liberty clause. Roe v. Wade (1973) built on this foundation to find a right to abortion. Though Roe was overturned in Dobbs v. Jackson (2022), the underlying privacy doctrine remains important for understanding how the Court interprets unenumerated rights — rights not explicitly listed in the Constitution.
Required SCOTUS Cases
11 cases
Schenck v. United States
1919
Can the government restrict speech that creates a "clear and present danger" even during wartime?
Yes. The Court unanimously upheld Schenck's conviction for distributing anti-draft leaflets during WWI. Justice Holmes established the "clear and present danger" test — the First Amendment does not protect speech that poses an immediate threat to public safety.

Engel v. Vitale
1962
Does state-sponsored prayer in public schools violate the Establishment Clause?
Yes. Even a non-denominational, "voluntary" prayer written and encouraged by the state crosses the line between government and religion. The state's official endorsement of any prayer violates the constitutional wall of separation between church and state.
1st Amendment · Est. Clause Full Case →
Tinker v. Des Moines
1969
Do students shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate?
No. Students wearing black armbands to protest the Vietnam War was protected symbolic speech. Schools can only restrict student speech if it causes "substantial disruption" to the educational environment — and silent, passive protest does not meet that standard.
1st Amendment Full Case →
New York Times v. United States
1971
Can the government prevent a newspaper from publishing classified information — the "Pentagon Papers" — on national security grounds?
No. The government cannot impose prior restraint on publication without an extraordinarily heavy burden of justification. The Nixon administration failed to meet that burden, and the papers were published. Prior restraint is the most serious First Amendment violation.
1st Amendment · Press Full Case →
Wisconsin v. Yoder
1972
Can a state compel Amish families to send their children to school past 8th grade when it violates their religious beliefs?
No. Wisconsin's compulsory education law, as applied to the Amish, violated the Free Exercise Clause. The state's interest in educated citizens did not outweigh the Amish community's centuries-old religious practice and the parents' right to direct their children's upbringing.
1st Amendment · Free Exercise Full Case →
Roe v. Wade
1973
Does the Constitution protect a woman's right to terminate a pregnancy?
Yes (at the time). The Court found a right to privacy in the 14th Amendment's liberty clause, which encompasses the abortion decision. The trimester framework gave states increasing authority as pregnancy progressed. Overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) — still heavily tested on AP exams.
14th Amendment · Privacy Full Case →
McDonald v. City of Chicago
2010
Does the 2nd Amendment right to keep and bear arms apply to state and local governments?
Yes. Building on District of Columbia v. Heller (2008), the Court incorporated the 2nd Amendment to the states through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause. Chicago's handgun ban was struck down. This is the most recent major selective incorporation case.
2nd & 14th Amendments Full Case →
Gideon v. Wainwright
1963
Does the 6th Amendment right to counsel apply to state criminal proceedings for felony defendants who cannot afford a lawyer?
Yes. Clarence Gideon, who wrote his appeal by hand from prison, won a unanimous Supreme Court ruling. The right to an attorney is fundamental to a fair trial. States must provide a public defender to defendants who cannot afford one — a cornerstone of American criminal procedure.
6th & 14th Amendments Full Case →
Miranda v. Arizona
1966
Must police inform suspects of their constitutional rights before custodial interrogation?
Yes. Ernesto Miranda confessed without being told he had the right to remain silent or the right to an attorney. The Court required police to give what became the Miranda warnings before any custodial interrogation. Confessions obtained without warnings cannot be used in court.
5th & 6th Amendments Full Case →
Mapp v. Ohio
1961
Does the 4th Amendment's exclusionary rule — which bars illegally obtained evidence — apply to state courts?
Yes. Dollree Mapp's home was searched without a valid warrant. The evidence found — obscene materials — was used to convict her. The Court reversed the conviction and incorporated the exclusionary rule to the states, meaning illegally obtained evidence cannot be used in any American court.
4th & 14th Amendments Full Case →
Brown v. Board of Education
1954
Does racial segregation in public schools violate the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment?
Yes. "Separate but equal" is inherently unequal. The Court overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) unanimously, finding that segregated schools generated feelings of inferiority that damaged Black children's educational opportunities regardless of physical facilities. The most important civil rights ruling in American history.

Required Foundational Documents
2 documents
📜
Emancipation Proclamation
Abraham Lincoln · January 1, 1863 · Required per EK 3.12.A.1
Issued as a war measure under Lincoln's commander-in-chief authority, the Proclamation declared enslaved people in Confederate states permanently free. It did not free enslaved people in Union border states — a deliberate political choice. It reframed the Civil War as a war against slavery and set the stage for the 13th Amendment. On the AP exam, know what it did AND what it did not do.
→
✉️
Letter from Birmingham Jail
Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. · April 16, 1963
Written while imprisoned for protesting segregation in Birmingham, Alabama, King responded to white clergy who called his protests "unwise and untimely." This letter is one of the most precisely argued defenses of civil disobedience ever written. King distinguishes just from unjust laws, responds to every counterargument made against the movement, and does it all from a jail cell without access to a library. Read it as argument, not just inspiration.

Key Amendments
8 amendments
1st
Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition
Congress cannot establish a religion, prevent religious practice, restrict speech or press, or stop peaceful assembly. The most litigated amendment in American history — all of Unit 3's First Amendment cases flow from here.
EK 3.1
2nd
Right to Bear Arms
The right to keep and bear arms shall not be infringed. McDonald v. Chicago (2010) applied this to the states. The Court held in Heller (2008) that this is an individual right, not just a collective militia right.
EK 3.2
4th
Search and Seizure
Protection against unreasonable searches and seizures; warrants require probable cause. The exclusionary rule (Mapp v. Ohio) means illegally obtained evidence cannot be used in court.
EK 3.2
5th
Self-Incrimination, Double Jeopardy, Due Process
You cannot be forced to testify against yourself (Miranda), tried twice for the same crime, or deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process. The foundation for Miranda warnings.
EK 3.2
6th
Right to Counsel, Speedy Trial, Jury
Accused persons have the right to a speedy trial, an impartial jury, to confront witnesses, and to have an attorney. Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) applied the right to counsel to the states.
EK 3.2
8th
Cruel and Unusual Punishment
Excessive bail, fines, and cruel and unusual punishment are prohibited. Applied in death penalty cases and debates about prison conditions and juvenile sentencing.
EK 3.2
13th
Abolition of Slavery
Slavery and involuntary servitude abolished except as punishment for a crime. Made permanent what the Emancipation Proclamation only partially achieved. Ratified in 1865 after the Civil War.
EK 3.4
14th
Citizenship, Due Process, Equal Protection
The engine of Unit 3. Citizenship by birth, due process applicable to states, equal protection of the laws. Every selective incorporation case runs through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause. Brown v. Board used the Equal Protection Clause. Know this amendment cold.

AP Exam Guide for Unit 3
WHAT GETS TESTED MOST
  • Selective incorporation — how and why it works
  • The 14th Amendment's two key clauses
  • The "substantial disruption" test from Tinker
  • Prior restraint and why it's almost never allowed
  • The difference between Establishment and Free Exercise clauses
  • Why Brown overturned Plessy — and what "inherently" means
  • Miranda warnings — what triggers them and what they require
COMMON STUDENT MISTAKES
  • Confusing civil liberties (protection FROM government) with civil rights (protection FROM discrimination)
  • Thinking the Bill of Rights automatically applies to states — it doesn't without incorporation
  • Saying Roe v. Wade is "still the law" — it was overturned in 2022 by Dobbs
  • Confusing Miranda (right to remain silent) with Gideon (right to a lawyer if you can't afford one)
  • Forgetting that Schenck's "clear and present danger" test was LIMITING speech, not protecting it
THE BIG PICTURE — HOW TO THINK ABOUT UNIT 3
Unit 3 is about the ongoing negotiation between individual freedom and government authority. Every case in this unit involves a conflict: the government wants to do something (censor the press, search a house, maintain segregated schools) and an individual or group claims a constitutional right prevents it. The Court's job is to decide who wins — and the answer almost always depends on which level of scrutiny applies and whether the government has a sufficiently important interest to justify the restriction. Learn the framework, not just the outcomes.
