Wisconsin v. Yoder
406 U.S. 205 · May 15, 1972 · Chief Justice Warren Burger
Unit 3 1st Amendment Free Exercise Clause ⭐ Frequently Tested
📋
BACKGROUND

Wisconsin law required children to attend school until age 16. Jonas Yoder and other Amish parents pulled their children out of school after 8th grade — around age 14 — in accordance with centuries-old Amish religious tradition. The Amish believe that high school education, with its emphasis on intellectual competition, individualism, and integration into mainstream society, directly threatens their religious community's survival and their children's prospects for salvation. Wisconsin prosecuted and fined the parents.

The parents argued the compulsory education law violated their Free Exercise rights. Wisconsin argued it had a compelling interest in educating its citizens. The Court had to weigh the state's interest in universal education against the Amish community's interest in religious preservation — and the children's interests in the middle.

❓
CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION
Does Wisconsin's compulsory education law, as applied to Amish families who object on sincere religious grounds, violate the Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment?
⚖️
THE RULING
✓ Ruled for the Yoders — 7 to 0
Unanimous on result · Chief Justice Warren Burger
"A way of life, however virtuous and admirable, may not be interposed as a barrier to reasonable state regulation of education if it is based on purely secular considerations; so too, few religious communities have established with such convincing force as the Amish the sincerity with which they hold their beliefs."
Wisconsin's law violated the Free Exercise Clause as applied to the Amish. Burger acknowledged the state's interest in education but held it was insufficient to override centuries-old Amish religious practice. Key factors: the Amish community was self-sufficient and law-abiding; Amish children were learning practical vocational skills; forcing high school attendance would expose children to values fundamentally at odds with their faith, threatening the community's survival. The state's interest in two additional years of formal schooling did not outweigh these specific religious concerns. Critically — this exemption applies only to religious communities with similarly deep, long-standing, and demonstrably sincere beliefs. It is not a general opt-out from school.
📐
PRINCIPLE ESTABLISHED
Sincere Religious Practice Can Override Neutral, Generally Applicable Laws
When a neutral, generally applicable law substantially burdens sincere religious practice, the Free Exercise Clause may require an exemption — particularly when the government's interest is not compelling enough to outweigh the religious burden. The Yoder test requires: (1) sincere religious belief (not merely philosophical), (2) substantial burden on that belief from the law, (3) government interest insufficient to override. Note: Employment Division v. Smith (1990) later narrowed this — holding that neutral, generally applicable laws do NOT require religious exemptions unless they specifically target religion.
🔗
LEGACY & CONNECTIONS
  • Engel v. Vitale
    1962    Engel addresses the Establishment Clause — government cannot sponsor religion. Yoder addresses Free Exercise — government cannot suppress religion. Together they define the two religion clauses of the 1st Amendment.
  • Employment Div.
    v. Smith 1990    Significantly narrowed Yoder. Scalia held that neutral laws of general applicability don't need religious exemptions — Oregon could deny unemployment benefits to Native Americans fired for using peyote in religious ceremonies. Yoder remains good law but only for the specific circumstances of that case.
  • Justice Douglas
    Dissent in Part    Douglas concurred except that the children themselves — not just the parents — should have had their views heard. Do Amish teenagers want this religious exemption? The case was decided without asking them. A remarkable early expression of children's independent constitutional interests.
⭐ AP EXAM TIP
Yoder is tested as the key Free Exercise case — know the distinction between Establishment Clause (Engel) and Free Exercise Clause (Yoder). The AP exam often presents scenarios about religious exemptions from general laws. Apply Yoder for sincere, long-standing religious practice vs. government regulation. Know that Employment Division v. Smith (1990) narrowed it — but Yoder is still the required case.

Common Mistakes
✗Confusing Free Exercise (Yoder) with Establishment Clause (Engel) — these are opposite problems.
✗Thinking Yoder creates a general religious exemption from all laws — it's very specific to sincere, deep-rooted religious communities.
✗Forgetting Employment Division v. Smith (1990) narrowed Yoder significantly.
